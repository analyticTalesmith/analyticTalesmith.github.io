<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="summary">Summary</h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li class=""><strong>Source Data </strong>— A two-table data set provided by <a href="https://mavenanalytics.io/data-playground" title="Maven Analytics">Maven Analytics</a> with over 422,000 records of a fictitious Canadian airline&#8217;s loyalty program members and the flights they took from April 2012 to December 2018.</li>



<li class=""><strong>Business Context </strong>— The airline ran a promotion from Feb. &#8211; Apr. 2018.</li>



<li class=""><strong>Problem </strong>— Did the promotion impact key performance and who did the promotion appeal to?</li>



<li class=""><strong>Importance </strong>— Anyone can run a promotion; not everyone can design a successful promotion that drives key performance. It is crucial to assess the effectiveness of marketing strategies and critically determine what did and didn&#8217;t work.</li>



<li class=""><strong>Insights </strong>—
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li class=""><em><strong>Promotion Attracted New Customers</strong> </em>— Both gross and net enrollment during Feb. &#8211; Apr. 2018 were significantly greater than would be expected without the promotion based on the growth trend prior to the promotion. Despite a peak in loyalty program membership cancellations in Aug. 2018, net enrollment has persisted at previous rates after the promotion end and active membership continues to steadily climb.</li>



<li class=""><em><strong>Promotion Drove Summer 2018 Sales</strong> </em>— 2018 Quarters 2 &#8211; 4 had increased flights compared to the same period in 2017.</li>



<li class=""><em><strong>Promotional Enrollment Customers Differed from Standard Enrollment</strong> </em>— The promotion was <em>especially </em>successful in Quebec and British Columbia. It was also popular among consumers who were divorced, women, and/or had attained at most a Bachelor&#8217;s degree, with each of these groups comprising more of the overall enrollment during the promotion than they did during standard enrollment.</li>



<li class=""><strong><em>Threat of Churn is Increasing </em></strong>— Gross enrollment trends upwards primarily because of the spike in enrollment during the promotion. Without the spike, gross enrollment is persisting at about 200 new members each month. However, the amount of cancellations each month is out-pacing new enrollments, and net enrollment is overall trending negatively.</li>
</ul>
</li>



<li class=""><strong>Recommendations </strong>—
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li class=""><strong><em>Data</em></strong> —
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li class=""><em>Add table tracking additional promotions and their key characteristics </em>— While these data can demonstrate overall success of promotional enrollment and subsequent ticket sales, they are ultimately limited. If additional data was tracked regarding characteristics of each promotion, more robust analyses could be performed to more reliably indicate how success was driven by the promotions versus other contextual factors (e.g., standard business growth) or even noise (e.g., random coincidences falling on the tail ends of normal distributions). More data regarding the characteristics of the promotions could also enable optimization analyses to help design more effective promotions.</li>



<li class=""><em>Collect cancellation data </em>— More and more members are deciding to cancel or not renew their membership. Understanding what is driving this growing churn is crucial to managing enrollment and creating programs to maintain active members.</li>
</ul>
</li>



<li class=""><strong><em>Business </em></strong>—
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li class=""><em>Monitor churn </em>— Higher counts of cancellation are expected as an organization has more active members. The problem arises when cancellation starts to outpace new enrollment. As it currently stands, cancellations are picking up in pace faster than enrollments. As long as net enrollment stays positive, the business will continue to see growth, but it is crucial to monitor these metrics to proactively implement strategies to avoid a decrease in active members.</li>



<li class=""><em>Explore Promotional Opportunities </em>— Something about the Q1 2018 promotion appealed to a subset of consumers, and it would be beneficial to assess whether that demographic&#8217;s persona is successfully represented in business decisions moving forward. Additionally, promotional enrollment members have similar (though marginally greater) customer lifetime values as standard enrollment members, but they tend to fly less and therefore cost the airline less in reward points. Additional promotions could boost enrollment and drive relationships with valuable customers.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="project-overview">Project Overview</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="preparing-data">Preparing Data</h3>



<p class="">The data comprised two tables: one tracking customer loyalty program membership (including demographics and enrollment details) and the other tracking loyalty program members&#8217; activity (including how many flights they took, points they earned, and points they redeemed each month). Unfortunately, the data on customer salary had unexpected negative values and the provided data dictionary/codebook simply documented the &#8220;Salary&#8221; column as &#8220;Annual Income&#8221; without additional information on this particular characteristic of the data. Because of this, I chose to disregard salary from the analyses. Otherwise, the data were cleaned well and appeared as expected.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="exploratory-data-analysis">Exploratory Data Analysis</h3>



<p class="">Given the business context and the available data, I knew my primary focus was going to be on how the membership program promotion impacted key performance indicators and the specific characteristics of promotional enrollment members compared to standard enrollment members. </p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="data-reshaping">Data Reshaping</h4>



<p class="">The loyalty program membership table is wide, but the flight information is long. <em>Very </em>long, even. I could quickly throw the membership data into simple PivotTables and get the information I wanted from them without any additional steps. But the flight info needed to be aggregated across each unique loyalty program membership ID number to explore the data I wanted. So that meant enabling the DAX add-on in Excel so I could use the Data Analysis Expressions to aggregate. I specifically used DAX to calculate the lifetime total flights, total miles flown, average flight distance, points earned, and cost of redeemed points for each program member. These aggregations were then inserted into the loyalty membership table for convenient access as a PivotTable field.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="visualization-verification-source">Visualization Verification Source</h4>



<p class="">With the data reshaped, I created PivotTables to explore descriptive characteristics of the data. The first goal was to simply figure out what story was embedded within the data. The second was to create a source to verify the accuracy of aggregations and visualizations I would later create in Tableau. </p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="tableau-data-visualization">Tableau Data Visualization</h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="importing-the-data-and-building-relationships">Importing the Data and Building Relationships</h4>



<p class="">I have already discussed the first two CSVs I imported into Tableau: the wide program membership information and the long monthly customer reports. These were simply related by the shared customer ID. But I quickly realized that if I wanted to use the date and month columns effectively for loyalty program enrollment and cancellation metrics/visualizations, I needed to create a helper CSV. So I went into Excel and wrote the function to generate the first day of each month present in the data set to generate a data scaffolding framework. I related this CSV of dates to the membership info using two calculated fields, shown below, to track whether a member was actively enrolled during each month or not (EnrollStart &lt;= Date &lt;= EnrollEnd)</p>
