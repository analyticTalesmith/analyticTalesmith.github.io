<p>In today’s world, everything is data and data is everywhere. The ability to extract valuable insights from raw data is a game-changer for businesses and other organizations. In this post, I discuss why data analytics matters, the value of data-driven decision making. I also provide a general overview of the data analysis process.</p>





<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Why Data Analytics Matters</h2>



<p>Technology makes it easier than ever for entrepreneurs and organizations to reach a wide audience. And with that technology comes access to more data than ever before. So what do you <em>do </em>with all of that data? Your answer might be to ignore it and hope you make the right call. But you&#8217;re missing out if you rely on intuition-based decision making. You might say you should dig through the data and see what trends you can find. If that&#8217;s the case, you’re a step or two in the right direction. But you can still get more value from your data.</p>



<p>Data analytics empowers organizations to make informed decisions based on evidence rather than intuition or guesswork. Data-driven decision making gives you the opportunity to develop strategies and solutions based on concrete evidence and facts. Note that analytics still takes a careful eye and a business sense. Statistical analyses leverage raw data into findings. An analyst still needs experience in a field to make sense of those findings and leverage them into actionable insights.</p>



<p>Organizations that leverage data analytics gain a competitive edge by uncovering trends, opportunities, and risks that might otherwise be hidden or even misconstrued by the human eye without the aid of analytics.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">How Analytics Can Help Organizations</h2>



<p>It’s difficult to truly explain all the ways that organizations can leverage analytics and data-driven decision making to improve operations. As I said, everything is data and data is everywhere. Analytics is the process of leveraging that data into actionable insights. Every facet of operations can benefit in <em>some </em>way from analytics.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Examples of Analytics in the Real World</h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">A/B Testing</h4>



<p>Do you want to determine which copy is better at converting new visitors into subscribers? Well, analytics can do that with A/B testing. You can split your traffic so each visitor sees one variation of the copy you want to test. By recording the conversion rates for each variation, you can compare which copy achieves your goal better. And ultimately, you can use this finding to implement the variation that drives more conversions. Or perhaps you discover that both versions are equally good or bad at converting visitors. This still gives you important findings to empower your data-driven decision making.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Clustering and Personalization</h4>



<p>Do you want to verify that your personas accurately reflect your customers? Or perhaps you want to determine what types of customers currently buy your product or service? Analytics can aid in customer segmentation and personalization. Analytic strategies can organize customers into similar clusters based on attributes like purchasing history, demographics, and website interaction metrics. This helps identify the different segments within your customers. In turn, this clustering allows you to develop strategies to appeal to each segment’s needs and preferences. Analytics can even help tailor marketing material, product recommendations, and other aspects of customer experiences based on which segment they belong to. This process of personalization can improve customer satisfaction and loyalty.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">People/HR Analytics</h4>



<p>Or perhaps you realize that there is an abnormal employee turnover in your organization; analytics can help you identify the driving factors. Are specific types of people more likely to quit? Perhaps analytics reveal that the quality of leadership isn’t equal across teams. A training program could be designed to improve management styles and strategies to bring all leaders up to the same level and reduce your turnover. Maybe employees don&#8217;t consider the compensation attractive, or think the benefits offered are vanity benefits more so than meaningful benefits they care about. People analytics, or HR analytics, can help you identify these problems and develop working solutions to cultivate a more engaged, productive, and satisfied workforce.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Analytics in Your Organization</h3>



<p>As I mentioned, these examples are only the tip <em>of the tip </em>of the iceberg when it comes to the value that analytics can add to your organization. Analysts can leverage almost any raw data into actionable insights with analytics. This can be seen by the diverse subfields within data analytics: financial, marketing, operations, business intelligence, healthcare, retail, HR/people, social media, fraud, and energy analytics, to name a few.</p>



<p>Despite the wide range of areas of expertise represented in data analytics, the largest dividing factor is business acumen and subject matter expertise within the field; the same general concepts underlie most analytics, regardless of the field. And these foundational analytics can generate data-driven insights that fuel innovation and drive organizational success.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">General Overview of the Data Analysis Process</h2>



<p>Everyone approaches data analytics differently, and ideally, the same person will even take different approaches across different analytics projects. Despite the importance of viewing analytics as an iterative, non-linear process, there are a few core stages that make up the analytics process. Regardless of the exact process an analyst might go through for a project, the stages of data analysis generally fall into one of a few steps:</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>defining objectives,</li>



<li>collecting and preparing data,</li>



<li>familiarizing yourself with the data as needed,</li>



<li>performing statistical analyses,</li>



<li>interpreting findings,</li>



<li>communicating findings with stakeholders,</li>



<li>communicating insights and recommendations based on the findings.</li>
</ol>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Defining Data Analytics Objectives</h3>



<p>The data analysis process generally begins with clearly defining the objectives and goals of the analysis. While this isn’t <em>technically </em>a necessary step in the process, you will find that an analyst can <em>quickly </em>get lost in the analytics without ever leveraging the data into actionable insights if they don’t know what they’re looking for.</p>



<p>Without the guidance of a clear objective, you also run the risk of generating a collection of spurious findings. While the concept <em>easily </em>warrants its own full discussion, a spurious finding is a statistically significant finding that isn’t driven by anything but coincidence and the random nature (often called “noise”) inherent to real world data. And these findings are risky because they can misguide the strategies you develop if you don’t take care to tease out what actually underlies and drives the trends your analyses reveal.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Collecting and Preparing Data</h3>



<p>The exact nature of gathering your data for analyses will depend on what data your organization has already collected as well as the engineering and management of collected data. However, you will ultimately need to access the raw data that is relevant to the objective you have established and the problem you are trying to solve.</p>



<p>This might mean constructing queries to pull relevant data that has already been collected, engineering new solutions to collect new data, or even identifying second- or third-party data sources you can acquire relevant data from. Regardless of the exact steps you need to follow to get the data, you will need data that are relevant to the problem you’re trying to solve as established by your objective, and you have to make sure it is prepared and cleaned removing any outliers or other problem data.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Familiarizing Yourself With the Data</h3>



<p>Before you start performing statistical analyses, you want to ensure that you are familiar with the data you are working with. You will often visit this stage numerous times throughout the life of a project.</p>



<p>Exploratory data analysis (commonly referred to as EDA) involves examining and visualizing data. EDA is a necessary tool in the analyst tool belt. You might perform EDA to verify that you have cleaned the data properly. You might use it to summarize and visualize data to get a sense of how the data are distributed. EDA can also help you get a sense of how complete your data are and whether there are additional data you might need to collect to strengthen your analyses.</p>



<p>You <em>could </em>technically also use EDA to develop hypotheses about relationships or trends exist in your data, but you should <em>ideally </em>have an idea of what you’re looking for in your data before you get to this stage (ideally starting to establish what you’re looking for when establishing your objective and refining that as you collect your data and understand what data you will have access to for your analyses).</p>



<p>EDA will ultimately help you confirm that your data have been cleaned properly and that your data don’t violate any assumptions of the analytic techniques you plan to use. It will also help you make sure that the data you are working with <em>are </em>the data you <em>intended </em>to work with. Once you are familiar with your data and understand the nuances of the data, you can move on to your statistical analyses.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Performing Statistical Analyses</h3>



<p>This stage is the bread and butter of analytics. This is the stage where you are testing for differences between groups, creating predictive models, segmenting data based on similarity, or whatever other analyses you need to perform to garner insight into trends in your raw data. This is another step of the process that you will often revisit throughout an analytics project.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Findings and Actionable Insights</h3>



<p>While it’s nice to know that there’s a significant difference between two groups or that you have 5 distinct clusters of clients, knowing is only half the battle. You must then interpret the results of your analyses to figure out what they mean for your organization. So what if these 5 groups exist? What do we <em>do </em>with that info? This is the stage where you draw on your own expertise and the expertise of team members and other stakeholders to provide actionable insights that underlie data-driven decision making. By this stage, you have leveraged raw data into findings; this is the stage where you leverage your findings into actionable insights through data storytelling with stakeholders.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Data Analytics and You</h2>



<p>Data analysis and statistics play a crucial role in organizational operations by empowering data-driven decision-making based on evidence and facts. Leveraging analytics allows businesses to uncover hidden opportunities, mitigate risks, and optimize performance across various facets of operations. Whether it&#8217;s through A/B testing for website optimization, customer segmentation for personalized marketing, or people analytics for talent management, data analytics adds tangible value to organizations by driving innovation and informed decision-making. Ready to unlock the power of data analytics for your organization? Stay tuned for more tips and insights in upcoming blog posts.</p>
